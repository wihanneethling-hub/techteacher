**When Experience Meets Innovation: The Leadership Challenge Every School Faces**.

One of the most challenging leadership dilemmas facing schools today is not about finances, managing parents or coping with yet another new piece of technology.

It's about people.

How do school leaders balance the wisdom and experience of veteran teachers with the energy and innovation of younger staff members?

On one side are teachers who have spent decades in the classroom. They understand curriculum, assessment, classroom management and the subtle art of building relationships with students. They carry a wealth of institutional knowledge that cannot be replaced.

On the other side are younger teachers who are often eager to experiment. They are comfortable with technology, open to new approaches and willing to challenge traditional ways of doing things.

Both groups bring tremendous value.

The danger lies in assuming that either group has all the answers.

Technology has amplified this challenge. Quite often, conversations about innovation become centred on devices, apps and platforms. Some teachers  lack the experience and pedagogical foundation to realise that technology on its own does not improve learning. A poorly designed lesson does not become better simply because an iPad or laptop is involved.

Effective technology integration must be driven by sound pedagogy.

The question school leaders should be asking is not, "How do we get our teachers to use more technology?"

The better question is, "What do we want learning to look like?" And this brings me to my most critical point - having a shared, clearly defined vision that everyone buys into needs to be in place before decisions are made about devices, software or professional development.

Where do we want to be in five years?

What skills do we want our students to develop?

When I walk into a classroom, what should great teaching and learning look like?

This then brings me to my next difficult question: Does EVERY teacher need to embrace technology in the same way?

The answer is rarely straightforward.

Students benefit from consistency, particularly when schools have invested significantly in a digital ecosystem. A completely fragmented experience can create confusion and undermine long-term goals. I have seen Grade 8 learners struggle to cope with going form :"No iPads in my lesson!" to a teacher that operates in a completely paperless world.

At the same time, forcing every teacher into the same mould can be just as problematic. Great teaching comes in many forms. Some of the most powerful lessons may involve sophisticated use of technology. Others may involve little technology at all.

Perhaps the goal is not uniformity, but alignment.

Teachers do not need to teach in the same way. They never will. But as a leader, you should expect them to be working towards a shared vision of learning.

The most successful schools are often those that strike a good balance between these two groups. Experienced educators who provide a solid pedagogical foundation and professional, institutional wisdom. Younger teachers contribute fresh thinking, technological confidence and new perspectives.

When these strengths are combined, schools do not have to make the choice between tradition or innovation.

They create a culture where experience informs innovation, and innovation keeps experience relevant.
